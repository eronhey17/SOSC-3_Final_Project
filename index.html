The Unseen Letter: What is Bi-Erasure?

Bisexuals, those romantically or sexually attracted to more than one gender, often face a unique form of marginalization called bi-erasure. This occurs when society invalidates, ignores, or misunderstands bisexuality, framing it as “just a phase” or “confusion.”

Despite being a large segment of the LGBTQIA+ community, bisexuals are underrepresented in media, neglected in research, and often excluded even in queer spaces. This advocacy aims to raise awareness, debunk myths, and promote equal representation and mental health support.

The Impact of Invisibility & Binary Pressure

The lack of acknowledgment and understanding has tangible negative consequences for the bisexual community.

Over 50% of LGBTQ+ Population

According to a study by the Human Rights Campaign (2020), bisexual people make up more than half of the LGBTQ+ population.

50%+

Higher Mental Health Risks

The same study reports higher rates of anxiety, depression, and suicidal thoughts among bisexual individuals compared to their gay or lesbian peers, often linked to bi-invisibility and discrimination.

Pressure to Conform

Robinson (2017) explains that bisexual people are often pressured to conform to binary norms ("either gay or straight"), leading to internalized biphobia and self-doubt.

Queer Archipelagos: Bisexuality in the Philippines

In the Philippine context, Santos and Tolentino (2019) in “Queer Archipelagos” discuss that biphobia is still common, even in progressive spaces. Bisexual individuals are often stereotyped as unfaithful or promiscuous, further marginalizing them in discussions of sexuality, health, and rights.

This highlights the need for culturally sensitive advocacy that addresses specific local manifestations of bi-erasure and biphobia.

Our Advocacy: A Call for Validity & Action

This movement is not just about visibility; it's about validity. Being bisexual is a complete, legitimate identity that deserves respect and support. We push for:

Integrate comprehensive and accurate information about bisexuality into school curricula and public health campaigns. This includes discussing bi-erasure, debunking myths, and showcasing diverse bisexual experiences.

Train mental health professionals to understand the unique stressors faced by bisexual individuals, including internalized biphobia and the impact of bi-invisibility. Ensure services are accessible, affordable, and affirming.

Call for and support media (film, TV, literature, news) that portrays bisexual characters and stories with depth, complexity, and accuracy, moving beyond harmful stereotypes and tokenism.

Advocate for the explicit inclusion of "bisexual" as a category in research, surveys, and demographic data collection. Ensure policies aimed at protecting and supporting the LGBTQIA+ community specifically address the needs of bisexual people.
